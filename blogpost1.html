Literature Survey

There is considerable research in the area of cloud storage and space management, and research is conducted regularly in this area. Different academia research in this aspect through different modes and research ideologies, from solving problems through technology to solving problems through user research, testing, and problem-solving through an interactive, dynamic design. The solution to the problem depends on the perceptions and ideas of different schools of thought.

In some papers, cloud storage is discussed from a technical perspective (Sussenguth, 1963), while in others, the user interface is discussed from a user-friendly and attractive perspective (Borysiewicz, A. & Netguru, 2021), online data visualisation is important for increasing the use of cloud storage (Tanahashi et al., 2010) and research is underway to identify the methods for testing and evaluating the ease of use process (Hui & See, 2015; Truong, 2017).

  • Technical Aspects

    • Research has shown that data retrieval and searching can be simplified by implementing tree structures to store data, essentially reducing the search time by O(log N) (Sussenguth, 1963).

  • Tests and Feedback

    • The use of pilot studies for conducting user studies has also been discussed in other studies, which provide evaluators with the opportunity to practice data collection and determine potential problems while testing the protocol and the instruments necessary to make key predictions. (Truong, 2017).

  • Knowledge of Design

    • (Hui & See, 2015) discusses how different cloud storage services are used and how Customisable UI could increase ease of use. In order to eliminate the misconception of "One-size-fits-all" theory, it discusses the inability of file storage websites to provide an enhanced user experience and the need for customizable UI.

  • Ideation of a Feature

    • Researchers have found that online visualisation services have been increasing in conjunction with growing cloud storage by casual users (Tanahashi et al., 2010). According to Tanahashi et al., visualisation of data stored in cloud storage is important. It was suggested that casual users should have access to an interactive interface that uses cluster searches and suggestive visualisations to improve their experience.

  • Problem-Solution relevance

    • Similarly, there is a blog discussing the need to redesign the Drive and reimagine the functionalities of a typical user (Borysiewicz, A. & Netguru, 2021). 61% of the users found search to be problematic, while 45.9% of people felt that sharing files was an area of concern, while at the same time, 36% of people felt that organising files was unsatisfactory.

These studies clarified our problem statement and supported our research objectives, as well as providing us with information about current academic research and the perspectives of various academics on the problem sphere we are involved with. They served as a foundation for further investigation and understanding of the issues at hand, as well as potential solutions.

Heuristic Evaluation

The first step in determining what was wrong with Google Drive was to use it. *Internal screaming* We decided to conduct cognitive walkthroughs in order to conduct heuristic analysis of Google Drive's existing services. I sat down and went over the basic task flows, noting down any link breaks or issues.

Heuristic evaluation is a process in which evaluators or experts use rules of thumb to assess the usability of an interface through independent walkthroughs, allowing the team to improve the product's usability from the start.

It was difficult to determine the next steps in some areas, and the number of clicks could be reduced in others. The homepage was a mess, as it was nearly unusable, with the majority of it empty and no dashboard, which is not what one would expect from a Storage drive.

In the heuristic evaluation, guided walkthroughs were used to highlight the good and bad aspects of the interface. Conducting a thorough analysis of the problem was the first step toward developing an online storage management solution. The heuristic evaluation can be found here.

We tallied the results and made a note of them so that we could deal with them properly when the time came.

Survey

The next step was to distribute assessment survey forms once a basic understanding of the issues had been established. Students between the ages of 17 and 24, who are among the most frequent users of the G-Suite environment, were the target audience for the survey assessment.

The medium was Google Forms, and the forms were distributed through peer WhatsApp groups. The survey began with demographic questions such as profession, frequency of use, and purpose of use, followed by Likert scale-based questions to identify the problems users were having with filing, user interface, and search algorithms, and finally, they were asked to provide feedback on future features they would like to see and their thoughts on problems identified by the team.

Competitive Analysis

For our project "Google Drive me Crazy," we conducted a competitive analysis to better understand our competitors and to identify our requirements. The study's main objectives were:

  • Determine the competition's main characteristics.
  • Determine the competition's strengths and weaknesses.
  • Determine where the existing solutions/technology have flaws.
  • Determine additional requirements for our website.

Each choice for the competitive analysis was made for a reason:

  • Indirect competitor: Windows File Explorer. This is a very popular file management programme.
  • After Google Drive, Dropbox has the second-largest user base.
  • Mega: It is thought to be a safer cloud storage option than Google Drive.
  • According to one of our sources, OneDrive is better than Google Drive.

We assessed and documented our competitors' strengths and weaknesses in order to develop the requirements for our website solution. We would draw inspiration from the study's positive findings while avoiding incorporating the study's weaknesses into our solution.

We wanted to avoid some negative aspects in our product, such as a cluttered landing page, hidden file information, recent tab inconsistencies, and sluggish search results.

We wanted to incorporate some positive aspects such as a clean and minimalistic UI, reduced user clicks, storage visualisation, a useful landing page, and a separate tab for share requests, among others.

Competitive Analysis

We established contact with those who filled out the survey form, gave consent to the Interview round, and took their interviews to identify their pain points, gather more qualitative data, and understand different scenarios where they face problems after we completed the survey and had the quantitative data to understand the depth of the problem. Within the same stakeholder group, ten people from India, Finland, the United Kingdom, and Kenya were interviewed in person or via Zoom call to gather relevant information, map scenarios, and understand different user personas.

There were four subthemes to the questions. The first subtheme dealt with their demographics, the second with their Google Drive usage patterns, the third with their pain points and issues, and the fourth with the project, their needs, potential pitfalls, and technological limitations. The goal of the interviews was to bridge the gap between expectations and the final product, resulting in a significantly shorter turnaround time. Surveys were used to gather information on needs at all levels, from the broadest to the most specific (Super Importance of Conducting Stakeholder Interviews in UX., n.d.). Persona and Scenario Mapping

In any design process, defining the personas is critical because it helps us understand for whom we are creating these products. The attributes of the users aid in the understanding of their psychology, which in turn aids in the determination of the features.

We created two personas that were similar but distinct, allowing us to reach a broader range of users who would use our service. We created multiple personas because our service was not designed for a single type of user.

We also created anti-users this time. Those who will not be using our service. The goal was to identify the types of users who are unlikely to change their habits or find comfort in the existing Google Drive, and who will most likely continue to use it.

We created scenarios for them where they might be willing to use our product to complete a task once we had the two personas. The personas were given a set of tasks to complete in order to identify the walkthrough process, key features, and suggestions, among other things.

This allowed us to identify the most important features that needed to be implemented first, as well as the features that are more likely to be used and should be prioritised.

Feedback

We are conducting usability testing through 3 main methods:

  • Cognitive Walkthroughs with Loop 11

    • A cognitive walkthrough is a method of evaluating a product's usability in a structured manner. It would be conducted on Loop11 platform, as we learnt from our research reading and evaluate the system.

  • System usability scale

    • Existing Google Drive and our product to be able to compare both the products on a more quantitative basis.

  • Structured Interview

    • We will conduct structured interviews and talk to different users for feedback on our product and then side by side make changes.

These 3 ways to gather feedback and usability testing were determined solely on the bases of a paper we referred to during our Paper presentation, where we were fortunate enough to find the right way we could assess our Redesigned service.
